What is Endometriosis?

Endometriosis is a chronic, inflammatory disease that affects women, often causing severe pelvic and period pain that can be truly debilitating. It can lead to fertility challenges and affects areas beyond the pelvis as well.

This misplaced tissue still responds to monthly hormonal changes - it thickens, breaks down and bleeds. But unlike normal uterine tissue, it has no way to leave the body.

The trapped tissue causes inflammation and forms growths called lesions, typically on reproductive organs and nearby tissues, though they can sometimes appear elsewhere in the body.

Endometriosis also comes in different forms - from stage one (minimal) to stage four (severe) - some endo stays close to the surface of affected areas, while other forms dig deeper into tissues.

Endometriosis occurs when tissue similar to that found in the endometrium—the lining of the uterus—grows outside the uterus.

This misplaced tissue typically develops on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, and the tissue lining the pelvis, though in rare cases it can spread beyond the pelvic region.

Communication

Words are powerful. They can comfort, inspire, or unintentionally hurt. For women living with endometriosis, respectful and compassionate communication can provide a sense of comfort and belonging. It is not about saying the perfect thing but about showing that we care and are willing to understand their journey.

Do Use respectful and accurate terms Refer to endometriosis as a 'condition', not a 'disease' to avoid negative connotations. Living with this condition must require a lot of resilience.
Don't Do not dismiss their pain Downplaying their experience invalidates their reality. Avoid saying 'Everyone has bad periods - it's normal.'
Do Validate feelings and experiences Acknowledge the challenges they face with empathy. Going through this sounds challenging, and I admire your strength.
Don't Do not imply they are overreacting It suggests they are exaggerating, which can feel dismissive. Avoid saying 'Are you sure it's that serious?'
Do Respect their boundaries Encourage open communication while respecting privacy. Let me know if there's anything you feel comfortable sharing about your condition.
Don't Do not push solutions It can come across as trivializing a serious condition. Avoid saying 'Have you tried yoga or herbal remedies?'
Do Educate yourself Demonstrate initiative to learn and be supportive. I've read about endometriosis. I'd like to understand more about how it affects you, if you are comfortable sharing.
Don't Do not be overly pitying It can feel patronizing and diminish their sense of agency. Avoid saying 'I feel so sorry for you - it must be terrible.'
Do Offer practical help Show care and compassion for the person. Can I help you with anything? Just let me know.
Don't Do not blame them for their condition It places unnecessary guilt and responsibility on them. Avoid saying 'Maybe if you exercised more, it would get better.'

Diagnosis

A confirmed endometriosis diagnosis requires laparoscopic surgery with tissue biopsy and microscopic analysis. While there are no definitive blood tests or imaging studies that can provide absolute confirmation, doctors can still make reliable clinical diagnoses without surgery.

Diagnosis begins with a thorough discussion of symptoms and medical history, followed by a physical examination to identify potential signs of endometriosis. Imaging studies like ultrasounds and MRIs help rule out other conditions, though they cannot detect all forms of the disease.

While endometriosis is staged from I to IV based on surgical findings, symptom severity often doesn't correlate with these stages—even minimal endometriosis can significantly impact quality of life.

The journey to diagnosis takes approximately 9 years for most patients since their first doctor visit (source), with about 3/4 of them receiving at least one incorrect diagnosis along the way. (source)

Symptoms

Endometriosis symptoms vary widely, with some experiencing severe pain while others have mild or no symptoms. The impact on daily life can be significant, affecting everything from work to personal relationships.

Chronic Pelvic Pain

Ongoing pain in the lower abdomen or pelvis that can come and go with the menstrual cycle. It ranges from mild to severe and can make everyday activities more difficult.

Menstrual Bleeding Abnormalities

Periods that are heavier, longer, or more painful than usual. Cramping can be intense, and bleeding may last beyond the typical cycle, often making it hard to keep up with daily activities.

Sexual Intercourse Pain

Discomfort during or after intimacy, sometimes worsening around periods. The pain can vary from mild to severe, making physical closeness challenging.

Fertility Challenges

Endometriosis can make getting pregnant more difficult by affecting reproductive organs. While it doesn't always lead to infertility, it can add uncertainty and stress to family planning.

Urinary and Bowel Discomfort

Pain or pressure when using the bathroom, especially during periods. This can happen when endometriosis affects the bladder or intestines, making everyday functions uncomfortable.

Irregular Bleeding

Spotting or unexpected bleeding between periods. It may be light or more noticeable, disrupting the body's natural cycle and adding to the unpredictability of symptoms.

Productivity Loss

Those days when pain makes working impossible add up quickly—both in missed work and lower performance when pushing through symptoms. This is the biggest hidden cost of endometriosis, reflecting careers put on hold and missed opportunities.

Surgery

When medications aren't enough, surgeries become necessary to remove growths and provide relief. This cost often repeats as many patients require multiple procedures throughout their treatment journey.

Monitoring Tests

Regular ultrasounds and blood tests are needed to check if treatments are working. These necessary checkups create ongoing costs that many don't expect.

Hospitalization

Some cases require hospital stays for complications like cyst ruptures or severe pain. This means emergency care, specialist visits, and extra medical bills.

Physician Visits

Finding answers often means seeing multiple doctors before finding helpful treatment. Each specialist appointment adds to the overall cost of managing the condition properly.

Other Health Care Costs

Beyond doctor visits, many women turn to medications, alternative therapies, or fertility treatments to manage symptoms and improve their quality of life.

Treatment

While there is no cure for endometriosis, treatments aim to manage pain and slow disease progression. Options include pain relievers, hormone therapy, and in severe cases, surgery to remove endometrial growths.

Challenges and Priorities

Many individuals face difficulties getting diagnosed, dealing with long-term pain, and navigating the impact of endometriosis on fertility and mental health. Awareness and better healthcare access are crucial to improving outcomes.
